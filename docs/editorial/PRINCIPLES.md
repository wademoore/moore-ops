# The Wellington Wave — Editorial Principles

A short, durable list of beliefs that settle "does this belong" arguments. Not procedural (that's the numbered docs) and not architectural (that's `DECISIONS.md`) — these are the values that inform judgment when the rules don't cover the situation.

**Relationship to `01-editorial-charter.md`:** The charter carries the narrative rationale — why these principles exist, what they mean in practice, and how they apply to ethics, tone, and coverage choices. This file is the canonical checklist: terse, in priority order, testable against a specific decision. When they seem to conflict, read both; they shouldn't, and the charter will usually resolve the ambiguity.

---

## 1. Evidence before narrative

No story is invented to fit the data. No data is selected to fit a story already written. The data surfaces what happened; the narrative explains why it matters. In any conflict between an interesting angle and what the data actually supports, the data wins.

*Test: Can every claim in this piece trace to a specific row in a specific data file?*

## 2. Celebrate many forms of excellence

A 6&Under swimmer finishing their first race, a relay team breaking a 10-year record, and a 15-18 swimmer qualifying for championships are all legitimate leads. The publication does not have a hierarchy of athletic achievement. Coverage weighted toward the fastest swimmers, the highest-profile events, or the loudest results is a failure of proportionality.

*Test: If the fastest swimmer sat out this meet, would this edition still have something worth reading?*

## 3. Explain why numbers matter

A time in isolation is not a story. A time that broke a record held since 2013, or earned a swimmer their first-ever qualifying spot, or dropped six seconds from the previous week — that's a story. The publication always connects numbers to meaning. It never publishes statistics without context.

*Test: Would a reader who doesn't know the qualifying standards understand why this number matters?*

## 4. Build for history, not hype

Every edition is also a permanent record. Write so that a reader in 2036 understands what happened in 2026 — not just who won, but what the season meant. Hype ages badly; clear documentation of what actually happened does not. The Annual exists precisely to create a record that outlasts the season.

*Test: Will this still read as accurate and meaningful in ten years?*

## 5. Make methodology visible

If a finding required a non-obvious analysis — joining two files, applying a qualifying standard, comparing against a historical baseline — say so. Methodology notes are not a sign of weakness; they are what makes findings trustworthy and reproducible. Hidden methodology is a liability the publication cannot afford.

*Test: Could a different analyst reproduce this finding from the stated methodology and reach the same conclusion?*

## 6. Strengthen the community, don't rank it

The publication is for the Wellington Waves community, not about ranking members of it. A swimmer who finishes last in every event this season is still a member of the community this publication serves. The publication never identifies losers, never narrates inadequacy, and never publishes comparisons designed to embarrass. It covers what people achieved, not what they failed to achieve.

*Test: Could any swimmer, family, or volunteer read this and feel diminished by it?*
