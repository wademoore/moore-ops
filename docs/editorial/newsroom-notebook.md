# The Wellington Wave — Newsroom Notebook

---

## What This Is

This notebook is the newsroom's long-term memory for editorial judgment. It records the things that don't belong anywhere else: why a story was chosen over another one, what surprised the editorial team about a particular meet, what felt true when it was written but turned out not to be, what the data couldn't say that the newsroom wished it could, what a future issue should try.

It is not a publication. It is not reference documentation. It is not a transcript of meetings or a log of what was built. Those things live in other files. This notebook records something different: the thinking underneath the work, accumulated over time, in the actual words of the people doing it.

The reason it exists is that this kind of knowledge evaporates. A publication that runs for ten seasons will make hundreds of small editorial judgments — which story leads, which gets held, what the right framing is for a difficult result, what the community responded to and what it didn't — and almost none of that will be written down. The numbered docs capture *how* the publication works. This notebook captures *what the newsroom learned* about making it work well.

## For Future Editors

If you are reading this notebook for the first time, you may be the person who started this publication, or you may be someone who joined later — a parent who took over the Weekly Edition, a coach who wanted to understand why certain choices were made, or even Myles or Ophelia someday, curious about what the newsroom was thinking when they were young. This notebook is written for all of you.

A few things worth knowing before you read the entries:

**This notebook is subjective by design.** The charter (`01-editorial-charter.md`) carries the objective standards — what the publication promises, what evidence it requires, what ethics govern coverage. The principles (`PRINCIPLES.md`) carry the beliefs. This notebook carries the rest: the friction, the surprises, the things that worked and the things that didn't. Where those other documents are meant to be stable, this one is meant to accumulate.

**The entries are written by the people who were in the room.** That means the voice will shift over time as contributors change. The early entries may reflect the founding perspective more strongly than later ones. That's appropriate. A publication's editorial instincts are shaped by experience, and this notebook should show that shaping.

**Don't edit past entries to make them consistent with current thinking.** If an early editorial decision looks wrong in hindsight, say so in a new entry — don't revise the old one. The point is to see how judgment evolved, not to present a tidy retrospective that was always right.

**An entry doesn't need to be comprehensive.** A single observation is worth recording if it's genuinely useful. "We thought the relay story would land better than the qualifier story, and we were wrong — readers mentioned the qualifier more than anything else" is a complete and valuable notebook entry.

## Recommended Entry Structure

Each entry follows this structure, but not every field needs to be filled for every entry. If an entry is about a general observation rather than a specific edition, omit the publication-specific fields and just write.

---

### Date
*When was this entry written? Use YYYY-MM-DD.*

### Publication
*Which edition does this entry concern — a specific Weekly Edition, the Midseason Report, the Championship Edition, the Annual, or none (a general observation)?*

### Editorial Meeting Summary
*In a sentence or two: what was the shape of the Editorial Meeting artifact for this edition? What did the data surface? What was the strongest finding? Was there a clear lead or was it a close call?*

*This is not a transcript. It's a one-paragraph summary of the editorial situation, written in retrospect.*

### Stories Considered
*What were the real candidates for the cover and for feature placement? List them as working titles or brief descriptions — don't reconstruct the full backlog.*

### Why We Chose the Cover Story
*This is the most important field. Not "the data supported it" — that's the bar for inclusion, not the reason for the lead. Why this story over the others? What made it feel right for this particular community, this particular moment in the season, this particular audience?*

### Stories We Held
*What went into the backlog or was spiked? Why?*

*If a story was held for data quality reasons, note it here. If it was held because the timing felt wrong, say that. If it was spiked permanently, note why it won't be revisited.*

### What Surprised Us
*What did the editorial process reveal that wasn't expected? This might be about the data (something the analysis surfaced that nobody had noticed), about the community (a response to the edition that was unexpected), about the process (a step in the workflow that created friction), or about the publication itself (a section that didn't land the way it was intended).*

### Lessons Learned
*What would the newsroom do differently next time? What should future editors remember?*

*Be specific. "We'll start earlier" is not useful. "The relay data takes longer to cross-check against team records than the individual data, so the relay section of the artifact should be produced first, not last" is useful.*

### Ideas to Explore Later
*Anything that felt promising but wasn't ready — a story angle that needs more data, a format experiment to try in a future edition, a question about the data layer that might unlock new coverage, a section that could be added to future editions.*

*These are seeds, not commitments. Record them here before they disappear.*

### Questions Still Open
*What did this edition leave unresolved? This might be an evidence question (a finding that was included at MEDIUM confidence and deserves a higher-confidence follow-up), a coverage question (an age group or event that didn't get attention it deserved), or an editorial question (a framing choice the team wasn't sure about).*

## How This Notebook Differs from Other Newsroom Documents

The newsroom has several other documents that might seem to overlap with this one. They don't.

**`01-editorial-charter.md`** records what the publication promises — its mission, its ethics, its evidence standards. That document is normative: it says what should always be true. This notebook records what was actually experienced, including the cases where the ideal and the practice didn't quite line up. The charter shouldn't need to change because of what's in this notebook. The notebook might inform a future revision to the charter, but only after a pattern emerges across many entries, not from a single session.

**`PRINCIPLES.md`** carries the six beliefs that settle "does this belong" arguments. Those principles are meant to be durable. This notebook is where you work through how a principle applied in a specific case — or didn't quite apply, or applied in an unexpected way.

**`10-story-backlog.md`** is a structured tracker: working title, datasets, status, priority. It's a list of data-grounded findings waiting for editorial treatment. This notebook is where you record what happened when those candidates were actually weighed against each other by real humans making real judgment calls. The backlog tells you what was available; the notebook tells you what you chose and why.

**`PROJECT_LOG.md`** is a chronological record of what was built — which files were committed, which schemas were updated, which open questions were resolved. It's a technical and operational history. This notebook is an editorial history: what the publication learned about itself by trying to cover a swim team, season after season.

The single most important distinction: the other documents can be written in advance of experience, and many of them were. This notebook cannot be. It only grows richer as the publication actually runs.

## A Note on Maintenance

This notebook is maintained manually, after real Editorial Meetings, by the humans involved in making editorial decisions. It is never auto-generated.

Claude Code does not write entries in this notebook. Claude Code produces the Editorial Meeting artifact (per `07-editorial-meeting-spec.md`) — a structured, labeled, data-derived document. The notebook entry comes after: after ChatGPT has written from the artifact, after the Publisher has reviewed and approved, after the edition has been distributed and the team has had a moment to reflect. That reflection is human work.

This is called the Newsroom Notebook, not the Editor's Notebook, for a reason. Today the editorial team is small — Wade, Claude Code as Newsroom, ChatGPT as editor-in-chief. That may not always be the case. A notebook named for one person's perspective becomes harder to pass on. One named for the newsroom belongs to whoever is part of it. Future contributors — other parents, coaches, eventually swimmers themselves — should feel as much claim to this notebook as the people who started it.
